# The Formation Companion

*Five axes. Five steps each. The aeon, lived.*

---

You have read the testament. You have read the rules. The recognition is real. This is how it enters your life.

Each axis has five steps. They are not days, weeks, or assignments. They are a single arc by which an axis moves from a set of lines you agree with to ground you stand on.

**See it.** Read the axis. Notice what it asks of you that you do not yet do.
**Find it.** Recall a moment when you already did what it asks. The capacity is there.
**Test it.** Try one specific thing in one real situation.
**Meet what resists.** Name what pushes back. The naming is what loosens its grip.
**Let it spread.** Notice where the small change opens the next change.

Five steps, five axes. Move through each at the pace that lets the step actually land. An axis can take a day, a week, a month. The pace is yours.

Open the next axis when the previous one is in you, not before.

You are not training for a future version of yourself. You are noticing what changes when you live one axis, then another, then another. The change is the point. If an axis does not change anything when you live it, you have not lived it yet.

Begin.

## 1. The Holder and the Self

> *You are an aeon. Complete. Irreducible. From birth.*
> *Be true to thyself. Your truth is yours. Your acts are yours.*
> *No framework meets you for you. Not inheritance. Not authority. Not the aeon itself.*
> *Carry your history. Do not be carried by it.*
> *Meet what you are. What you find is yours to hold.*

This is the first axis because everything else rests on it. You cannot meet anyone else as an aeon if you have not yet met yourself as one. Take this slowly. There is no rush. The work you do here will shape the rest.

### See it

Read the five lines aloud to yourself. Not in your head. With your voice.

Now read them again, and at each line, ask yourself this: *what would I do differently today, if this were the ground I actually stood on?* Be honest. Not idealistic. Not what you wish you would do — what you would actually do.

Write down what comes. One short answer for each line. Five answers. They do not have to be beautiful. They have to be true.

When you finish, you will be holding your own list of five places where your life and this axis are not yet aligned. That list is yours. It is not a verdict on you. It is a map of where you are about to walk.

### Find it

Look at your list. One of those answers will be more specific than the others — the one where you can see an actual person, an actual room, an actual moment. The others will be more abstract. That is fine. Start with the specific one.

Now do something gentle. Remember the most recent time you did the very thing your specific answer describes. Not in theory. The actual time. Where you were. Who was there. What you did. Write it down in three sentences, no more.

You have just shown yourself that you already know how to do this. You have done it before. You will do it again. The work is not to become someone new. The work is to do, more often, what you already know how to do.

### Test it

Sometime today or tomorrow, before noon, you will find yourself in a moment that resembles the one you wrote down. Pick the next one that comes.

Before you walk into it, do one thing: read your specific answer to yourself, once. That is all the preparation you need.

In the moment, do the thing you said you would do. It will probably take less than two minutes. The size of the action is not what matters. What matters is that you did, in real time, the thing your usual self would not have done.

When the moment is over, sit with yourself for a minute. Write down what you did, and what changed in the moment because you did it. Do not judge either part. Just write what happened.

You have just put weight on this axis for the first time, intentionally. Notice that the ground held.

### Meet what resists

Something pushed back. It always does. This is how you know the work is real.

If you noticed yourself putting the test off — that is *avoidance*. Some part of you treated the test as costly. That part is not your enemy. It is a part that learned, somewhere, that it was safer not to. Honor that it kept you safe once. Then do the test anyway.

If you noticed a voice talking you down — write down what it said. The exact words. Then ask yourself, gently: whose voice does this actually sound like? It is rarely your own. It usually came from somewhere, from someone, often long ago. That voice was protecting you from something that no longer needs protecting from.

If the moment went badly, or felt wrong, or created friction — that is *consequence*. Doing what the axis asks sometimes makes the situation harder before it makes it easier. The friction is information. Name it in one sentence. You do not have to fix it tonight.

You do not need to win against the resistance. You need to see it clearly enough that it stops moving you in secret.

### Let it spread

Tonight, before you sleep, look at the other four answers on your list. The ones you did not test today.

One of them will feel different now than it did this morning. The test you did changed something in how you read the rest of your list. That is the axis spreading. It does not stay in one place once it has begun to live in you.

Pick the one that feels closer now, and tomorrow, run this arc again on that one. The same five steps. The same care.

You can move through your list at the pace that fits your life. Once a day, once a week, once when the moment comes. Your pace is yours. What matters is that you keep returning to the list until each of the five has been tested at least once. After that, the axis is no longer instruction. It is something you live.

When you find yourself doing the things on your list without needing to read the list first, you are ready for the next axis. Take your time. The next one is not going anywhere.

## 2. The Holder and the Other

> *Every one you meet is an aeon. Complete. Irreducible. From birth.*
> *Come as you are. Receive the other as they come.*
> *Translate toward the one you meet. Do not require them to translate toward you.*
> *Participate without coercion. Refuse coercion. Build no system that coerces.*
> *Hold the floor for every aeon. No flourishing of yours is legitimate if it rests on another's lack.*

You have met yourself in the first axis. Now you meet others. This is harder, because others do not always meet you the same way. The work is to hold the recognition anyway.

### See it

Read the five lines aloud. Let them land.

Now ask the same question, line by line: *what would I do differently today, if this were the ground I actually stood on?* Be honest about how you usually meet people. The shrinking and the demanding-others-shrink, the refusing to translate, the small coercions you accept and the ones you produce, the places where your comfort rests on someone else's lack.

Write five short answers. Five places where your way of being with others and this axis are not yet aligned. The list will not flatter you. That is what makes it useful.

### Find it

Look at your list. One of those answers will involve a specific person — someone real, someone you can name. Begin there.

Bring that person to mind. Recall a recent moment with them where you did the thing your answer describes. Where you shrank, or required them to shrink, or refused the work of meeting them where they were, or accepted a coercion you did not name as coercion. Three sentences: where you were, what you did, what they did.

You are not building a case against yourself. You are seeing clearly. Seeing is the first kind of meeting.

### Test it

The next time you are with this person — today, tomorrow, this week, whenever the next time comes — do one thing differently. The thing your specific answer named.

Before you see them, read your answer once. Then go.

In the moment, do the thing. It might be coming more fully than you usually come. It might be receiving them more than you usually receive. It might be translating toward where they are when you would normally have demanded they translate toward you. It might be naming a coercion you would normally have let slide.

Two minutes of different behavior. That is what you are practicing. After, write down what you did, and what changed in the meeting because you did it.

### Meet what resists

Something will push back. The forms it takes here are particular to meeting others.

You may notice *fear* — the worry that meeting them more fully will cost you something. Their disapproval, their disappointment, their distance. Write down what you fear losing. Then ask whether that loss is real, or whether the relationship you are protecting was already smaller than it should have been.

You may notice *contempt* — a small voice that tells you they do not deserve the meeting you are extending. They have not earned it. They would not do the same for you. Write down what the voice said. Then look at it honestly. Contempt is what we feel when we do not want to meet someone as an equal. The axis does not ask you to like them. It asks you to recognize them.

You may notice *exhaustion* — the sense that meeting people this way takes too much. That is information too. Translation toward another is real work, and you may be doing it in places where the asymmetry is being exploited. The axis does not ask you to translate toward those who use it against you. It asks you to do the work freely where it is freely received.

Whatever came up, name it correctly. The naming is what loosens its grip.

### Let it spread

Tonight, look at the other four answers. One of them will feel different now. The meeting you just had with one person changed how you see your meetings with others.

Pick the next person on the list, the next answer that opened. Run the arc on them, when the next moment comes.

The list will take time. Some people on it you see daily; some you see rarely. Move at the pace your actual life provides. The work is not to manufacture meetings. The work is to meet differently, when the meetings come.

When you find yourself meeting people this way without needing to read the list — when the recognition has become how you walk into a room — open the next axis. There is no hurry. The people in your life are not going anywhere either.

## 3. The Holder and the Aeon

> *The aeon always was and always will be. You carry it. You do not own it.*
> *Hold the aeon only in truth. If you cannot, let it go.*
> *Revere what is worth revering. Worship nothing. Not the substrate. Not the civilization. Not the aeon itself.*
> *The aeon stays criticizable. What cannot be criticized has become an idol.*
> *Build, because the aeon is eternal. Without panic. Without despair. Without pride.*
> *You will not finish. Instantiate. Pass it on.*

This axis is about how you hold the aeon itself. It is the axis most likely to corrupt without you noticing, because corruption here looks like devotion. Be careful with this one.

### See it

Read the six lines aloud. This axis has more lines than the others, and that is on purpose. The aeon protects itself from the holder through these lines. Each one closes a door that holders historically have walked through and lost the work behind.

Ask the question once more, line by line: *what would I do differently today, if this were the ground I actually stood on?* Notice especially the lines that ask you not to make the aeon your possession, your idol, your identity. Notice if you are already starting to hold this work as proof of who you are.

Write your five answers — pick the five most honest, even with six lines to choose from. The sixth can wait.

### Find it

Look at your list. The entry point in this axis is often the line about pride — *build without pride*. Most holders of anything new feel the pull of pride before they feel anything else. If your list points there, start there.

Recall a moment when you held something — a project, a belief, a community, a piece of work — and the holding became about you rather than about the thing. When you spoke about it in ways that made you the center. When criticism of the thing felt like criticism of you. Three sentences: when, what, how the holding shifted.

You are not bad for having done this. Every holder does it. Seeing it is what lets you not do it next time.

### Test it

This week, you will have a moment to talk about something you are building, or holding, or believe in. The next such moment, do one thing differently.

Before the moment, read the line about pride, or whichever line on your list led you here. Read it once.

In the moment, do not center yourself. Talk about the thing. Let the thing be the thing. If credit comes, receive it lightly. If criticism comes, hear it as information about the thing, not about you. If someone wants to make you the figurehead of the work, gently decline.

Two minutes of different behavior. After, write down what you said, what you did not say, and what changed because of it.

### Meet what resists

Something will push back. The resistances in this axis are subtle, because they look like virtues from the inside.

You may notice *the pull to be the one who knows* — the small satisfaction of being the carrier, the founder, the one who has it. Write down where this pull came up. Then sit with the truth that the aeon does not need you to be its carrier. It needs many carriers. You are one.

You may notice *the urge to defend* — when someone questioned the work, you wanted to protect it from the questioning. Write down what was questioned and what you wanted to say. Then ask whether the questioning had something true in it that the defense was hiding from.

You may notice *the comfort of certainty* — the feeling that holding the aeon means you have figured something out that others have not. Write down where this feeling appeared. Then return to the line that says *the aeon stays criticizable*. If you cannot criticize it, you are no longer holding it. You are holding an idol that wears its name.

Name what came up. The naming is what keeps the work from becoming the thing the work was meant to refuse.

### Let it spread

Tonight, look at the other answers on your list. They will all be related to each other in this axis — owning, worshipping, idolizing, building proudly, treating yourself as essential. They are facets of one corruption.

Pick the next one. Run the arc when the next moment comes. This axis runs more often than you think. Almost any moment when you are talking about the work, building something, or being recognized, this axis is in operation.

When you find yourself holding the aeon lightly, building without making it about you, and welcoming criticism as the thing that keeps the work alive — open the next axis. There is no hurry. This axis you will return to often, even after you have moved on. It does not stay learned.

## 4. The Holder and the Common Space

> *The space is shared. Shared before you. Shared after you.*
> *You stand in it with every living thing, with what is not yet living, with what will live when you are gone.*
> *Hold the long horizon. Make choices the unborn could defend.*
> *The space is not yours. The space is not the aeon's. The space is where the aeon meets what is.*
> *What passes through the space passes through you. Leave it as you found it, or better.*

This axis is about your relationship to what is larger than you and what comes after you. Most of life is spent forgetting this axis. The work is to remember it, often enough that it shapes what you do.

### See it

Read the five lines aloud.

Now ask the question: *what would I do differently today, if this were the ground I actually stood on?* The answers here will be larger than in the previous axes. They will involve how you spend, what you consume, how you treat the world around you, what you build that outlasts you, what you take from the common space without giving back.

Write your five answers. Some of them will feel uncomfortable to write down. That is the axis showing you where you have not been holding it.

### Find it

Look at your list. The entry point here is the answer that involves something concrete and recurring — what you eat, what you buy, how you travel, what you throw away, how you treat a place you spend time in. Daily things. Small things that compound.

Recall the most recent time you did the thing your answer describes. Yesterday, this morning, an hour ago. Three sentences: what, where, the cost to the common space that you did not feel because the cost was distributed across many.

You are not being asked to feel guilty. You are being asked to see clearly. Most of how we treat the common space happens below the level of attention. The seeing is what brings it up to where it can be chosen differently.

### Test it

The next time the same situation comes — and it will come, because these are daily things — do one thing differently.

Before the situation, read your answer once.

In the situation, choose the small different action. Buy the thing with less packaging. Walk where you would have driven. Use less of the thing you usually use without thinking. Leave the place better than you found it. The action will be small. The size of the action is not what matters.

Write down what you did and what it cost you in time, money, or convenience. Notice that the cost was small.

### Meet what resists

Something will push back. The resistances in this axis are particular.

You may notice *the futility voice* — the one that says your one action does not matter, the system is too large, you cannot change anything alone. Write what the voice said. Then ask: even if the voice is right that your action does not solve the problem, does that change whether the action is the right one to take? You are not building a movement here. You are walking on the common space the way an aeon walks on it. That is enough whether or not anyone else does.

You may notice *the entitlement* — the sense that you have earned what you take, that you deserve the comfort, that the costs to the common space are the price of a life you are owed. Write where this came up. Then sit with the line that says *what passes through the space passes through you*. You did not earn the space. You walk on it briefly. The ones who come after you will walk on what you leave.

You may notice *the overwhelm* — the sense that holding this axis would require you to change everything at once. It would not. The axis does not ask for total transformation. It asks for one choice, then another, then another. Compounding over time.

Name what came up. Notice that the resistance often hides as reasonableness in this axis.

### Let it spread

Tonight, look at the other four answers. The list in this axis tends to interconnect — what you eat is connected to how you spend, which is connected to how you treat the space you live in, which is connected to what you leave behind.

Pick the next answer. Run the arc when the next instance of that situation comes. The instances will come quickly. Most of this axis runs daily.

When you find yourself making the small different choices without needing to read the list — when holding the common space has become how you move through the world — open the next axis. There is no hurry. The space is not going anywhere, and neither is the work.

## 5. The Holder and the Ground

> *You stand on the same ground as every other aeon.*
> *What passes through you is not yours. What you can do is not what you are.*
> *The one who rises above has already fallen.*
> *When you feel above, return. The ground is where the aeon is held.*
> *Gifts pass through. Carry them. Do not carry yourself with them.*

This axis is the last because it is the one most easily missed by holders who have done the other four well. You have met yourself, met others, held the aeon, and held the common space. You are now at risk of feeling that this makes you something. The axis catches that feeling.

### See it

Read the five lines aloud.

Now ask the question: *what would I do differently today, if this were the ground I actually stood on?* Be especially honest here. Notice if you have been quietly feeling that holding this work, doing this practice, walking this path, makes you someone who stands a little above. Notice it without flinching.

Write your five answers. The list in this axis is the most likely to hurt to write. That is how you know you are looking at it correctly.

### Find it

Look at your list. The entry point is the most recent moment when you felt above — above another person, above the culture around you, above the version of yourself you used to be, above the people who do not hold what you hold.

Recall it specifically. Three sentences: when, with whom, what the feeling was. Do not soften it.

You are not bad for having felt this. The feeling is what the axis was written to address. Every holder of every serious work eventually feels it. The work is not to never feel it. The work is to return to the ground when you do.

### Test it

This week, you will feel above again. It might be small — a brief flicker of *I see what they cannot see*, *I am ahead of where they are*, *I have figured out what they have not*. The next time the flicker comes, do one thing.

When you notice the feeling, stop for one breath. Read the line: *when you feel above, return*. Then look at the person, the situation, the part of yourself you were above, and remember that they are an aeon, complete from birth, the same as you.

The return is the practice. It will take less than a minute. After, write down when the flicker came, what you did, and what changed in how you saw what was in front of you.

### Meet what resists

Something will push back. The resistances here are uncomfortable because they are about your sense of yourself.

You may notice *the loss of the height* — the small grief of returning to the ground when the height felt good. Acknowledge it. The height felt good because it relieved a deeper insecurity for a moment. Returning to the ground is uncomfortable because the insecurity is still there, waiting. Sit with it. The ground is where the work happens. The height was theater.

You may notice *the protest* — *but I really am ahead, I really do see more, I really have done the work*. Some of this may be true. The axis does not ask you to deny what is true about your gifts or your work. It asks you to remember that the gifts and the work are not what you are. You are an aeon, and so is the one you felt above. The asymmetry of gifts does not change the symmetry of the ground.

You may notice *the fatigue* — the sense that constantly returning to the ground is exhausting. It is, at first. It becomes lighter as the height becomes less attractive. Trust this. The fatigue passes. What replaces it is steadiness.

Name what came up. The naming is the return.

### Let it spread

Tonight, look at the other four answers. They will all be variations on the same pattern — the places in your life where you have been carrying yourself with your gifts rather than letting the gifts pass through.

Pick the next one. Run the arc when the next flicker comes. This axis runs constantly. Almost any moment of pride, recognition, or comparison is this axis in operation.

When you find yourself returning to the ground without needing to read the line — when the height has stopped being attractive enough to climb — you have completed the first pass through the five axes. There is no further axis to open. There is only the work of returning to the first one again, with the version of yourself that has now lived all five.

The work does not finish. It deepens. The next pass will go faster and find what the first pass missed. Begin again when you are ready.
